## How it works under the hood

Two common approaches:

- **Keyword and skill matching**: the model extracts skills, years of experience, and seniority signals from the CV and scores against the role’s must-haves. Fast and explainable.
- **Embedding-based matching**: the model converts CV and [role description](https://join-staging.com/en/glossary/job-description) into vectors and scores semantic similarity. Catches relevant experience the keyword approach misses (a “lead React developer” matches “senior frontend engineer”).

Most modern ATS systems combine the two.

## What it does well

- **Compresses CV review time** from 1-3 minutes per [application](https://join-staging.com/en/glossary/application-form) to 10-20 seconds for the reviewer to confirm the AI’s read.
- **Surfaces relevant candidates** the human reviewer might skip because of a non-standard career path or under-marketed CV.
- **Stays consistent** — applies the same criteria to candidate #1 and candidate #100.

## Where it fails

- **Bias inheritance**: if the model was trained on historical hiring decisions and those decisions were biased, the model reproduces the bias at scale.
- **Off-spec creativity**: AI [screening](https://join-staging.com/en/glossary/screening) over-weights conventional CVs. A career-changer with strong skills but unconventional path can score low.
- **Gaming risk**: candidates aware of AI [screening](https://join-staging.com/en/glossary/screening) optimize their CVs against it. The signal degrades.

## How to use it sensibly

Treat AI screening as a ranking aid, not a filter. The human reviewer still sees every [application](https://join-staging.com/en/glossary/application-form); the AI score reorders the queue. Anything auto-rejected is a process risk.
